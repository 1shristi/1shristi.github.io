Case Study

Revamping the Dashboard

Transforming a parking management app through user-centered design and strategic information architecture

Role Solo UX Designer
Timeline 4-5 Weeks
Client Parking Site Management
Overview

The Challenge

The client's parking site management app worked well technically, but when presenting to executives for buy-in, UI elements like the sparse dashboard and bloated navigation menu made it challenging to showcase the app's value proposition effectively.

The Approach

In an interview workshop setting with the client, I facilitated and adopted the perspectives of two key individuals:

  • Subject matter expert & Product owner — to understand the underlying business logic and its product goals and value proposition.
  • New user — to define user flows of daily users, what problems they are trying to solve and why it's needed.
Key Insight

This approach revealed the core usability issues that my user interface needed to address, especially on the main dashboard. The client could participate in the design solution and help narrow the project scope to just pertinent screens and user flows.

Solution

Design Decisions

allow for gradual reveal · assess content hierarchy · reorder sidebar navigation

Map as anchor for all user flows

The map is the first element users see on the updated Dashboard. It's intentionally laid out to visually reflect the physical space that site managers work with in their mental model.

  • Apply affordances to guide users from one dataset to another
  • Find opportunity to use spatial assets to reduce information overload
  • Use visuals and typography to organize different types of menu item content
Turns out it's the other way around — users think location-first, not data-first.

Users manage multiple parking locations, and therefore they think and complete tasks in terms of one unique location at a time. The job the user has to do is the same across parking locations but the data set is unique to that specific location.

Impact

This discovery helped me better understand what value the data had for users, so I could decide which elements made sense to reveal on the dashboard, at what point, and which ones could be stored away.

Challenge #1

Cluttered Sidebar Menu

The original menu had 13 items and little affordances to inform users of what tasks or activities are within each one. Through systematic analysis, I reduced the menu by 50%.

1

Map, question, define menu pages

I questioned the purpose of each screen, what users could see and do, why the information was valuable, and whether actions needed to be performed beforehand.

2

Learn, clarify, define with the client

With the purpose of each screen defined, I constructed the relationship between product, people, and the system and where the intended business goals fit.

3

Prioritize screens by usefulness and ease

I analyzed and categorized each menu item by task priority and number of actions required, revealing which features belonged on the dashboard versus nested menus.

Before
After

Visual Floorplan Interface

Drawing from the physical clipboard task that site managers used daily, I designed an interactive floor plan that mimics their real-world workflow. This reduced cognitive load by aligning the digital interface with their existing mental model.

Reflection

Key Learnings

Extra solutions can quickly become problems

This project taught me to thoroughly question whether a new design feature truly simplifies the user's process or just creates additional overhead. The best solutions often remove complexity rather than add new features.

Understanding mental models drives better design

By discovering that users think location-first rather than data-first, I was able to restructure the entire information architecture around their natural workflow, resulting in a more intuitive and efficient interface.
