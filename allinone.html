All-in-one shipping and marketplace solution

to replace a broken legacy system

overview small team | lead designer | 4 weeks

The client had a comprehensive product roadmap to replace an outdated, monolithic legacy system used for shipping international cosmetic goods, which had become a bottleneck over time causing inefficiencies and staff frustration.

I was brought in as a contractor to lead user research and a design for the new shipping platform interface in order to translate critical legacy system flows into efficient, user-friendly UI screens that could handle the complexities of international shipping within the client's permissioned B2B marketplace.

proposal


I proposed 4 dedicated weeks using an iterative design process—cycling through research, brainstorming, production, and testing over four weeks—to create UI screens for presentation to key legacy system stakeholders.

Why just 4 weeks?

The client had a tight deadline for the entire product development and wanted to leverage a visual solution as soon as possible to open the discussion on budgeting and resources for the entire project.

From past experience, I knew the scope of the project could easily extend the original timeline so I made sure the team understood that design resources had to be a priority for the duration of the 4 weeks.

  • Quick access to: Subject Matter Experts (SMEs)
    I expressed the need to have easy and quick access to subject matter experts (SME). These were senior staff members who had been using the legacy system for years and had a deep understanding of its functionalities and limitations.
  • Alignment with Product Owner (PO) and core team
    Given the deadline, I requested daily meetings with the PO and regular meetings with the team in order to understand their UI vision for the product; including time to read and ask questions from their research documents.

1 Research process details

Analysing application, service safari method

Following along the application's SOP (Standard Operating Procedures) documents, I used the service safari method to gather insights on how users interacted with the system in real-world scenarios.

Research discovery: the legacy system is not important and should be ignored completely. Instead, defining the users' specific actions and physical touchpoints will better inform the design timeline

Gathering physical documents, noting touchpoints

Physical documents were not only noted as touchpoints but also as opportunities to be transcribed into the user interface for a better user experience.

Research discovery: physical documents were used only when the computer was not accessible and were prone to errors.

Contextual, 1:1 semi structured, ethnographic interviews

There was no specific direction for how interviews were to be conducted. I began all interviews as a personal understanding of the kinds of tasks the users completed and made a note of how many of these tasks were painpoints attributed to the legacy system vs the struggles of the overall role and responsibilities.

Research discovery: many of the user tasks that were described were not necessarily essential to complete, instead they were "work-around solutions" for dealing with the legacy system.

2 Prototype process details
Flow charts define UI components

Task statements define UI components of the interface and each statement maps to standardized universal UI component(s).

"The warehouse operator parcel dimensions."
→ Editable component

"Quantity is physically checked against online data..."
→ Search toolbar

"Printing labels need to include the following information..."
→ Setup wizard

Draft designs are for agreement

Product Owners think about the product as much as users value the tasks it's meant to support; it's their baby, and aligning the initial interface with POs' and stakeholders' expectations and vision is often more important early on.

I treat them like contracts for visual agreement.

For example, I'll introduce the PO to the initial layout and structure by showing an unpolished, high-level interface that highlights only the major tasks and where they may fit.

Paper sketches

"Viewing items"

"Seleting parcels"

"Dragging and dropping"

I start with paper sketches and treat them like private notes. It's akin to playing in a personal sandbox with no expectations and lots of intuition. I draw out others' ideas and translate conversations into components for my own understanding.
