All-in-one shipping interface

to replace the broken legacy application

OVERVIEW

small team | lead designer | 4 weeks

The Problem

Due to a lack of consistent updates over time, my client's legacy system for shipment had become bloated and a bottleneck causing inefficiencies that negatively impacting their bottom line.

With a comprehensive product roadmap for the new shipping interface ready to go, I was brought in as a contractor to lead user research and design by defining, adapting, and applying user flows from the legacy system into an efficient user-friendly interface. Within the MVP scope, the interface was expected to support international shipping within the client's permissioned B2B marketplace.

PROCESS

Proposal

The Timeline

I presented a timeline of four dedicated weeks following the standard design process which cycles through research, ideation, prototyping, and testing. The end result would be a series of UI screens ready for presentation to the stakeholders and c-executives of the legacy system.

Why just 4 weeks?

The client wanted to leverage a visual solution to positively influence and steer the discussion towards budgeting and resourcing for the entire product roadmap in the fourth quarter.

From past experience, I knew the scope of the project could easily extend the original timeline so I made sure the team understood that design resources had to be a priority for the duration of the 4 weeks.

Requirements for Success
  • Quick access to Subject Matter Experts (SMEs): I expressed the need to have easy and quick access to subject matter experts. These were senior staff members who had been using the legacy system for years and had a deep understanding of its functionalities and limitations.
  • Alignment with Product Owner (PO) and core team: Given the deadline, I requested daily meetings with the PO and regular meetings with the team in order to understand their UI vision for the product; including time to read and ask questions from their research documents.

Research methods used

1. Gathering physical documents, noting touchpoints

Physical documents were not only noted as touchpoints but also as opportunities to be transcribed into the user interface for a better user experience.

Research discovery: physical documents were used only when the computer was not accessible and were prone to errors.

2. Contextual, 1:1 semi structured, ethnographic interviews

There was no specific direction for how interviews were to be conducted. I began all interviews as a personal understanding of the kinds of tasks the users completed and made a note of how many of these tasks were painpoints attributed to the legacy system vs the struggles of the overall role and responsibilities.

Research discovery: many of the user tasks that were described were not necessarily essential to complete, instead they were "work-around solutions" for dealing with the legacy system.

3. Analysing application, service safari method

Following along the application's SOP (Standard Operating Procedures) documents, I used the service safari method to gather insights on how users interacted with the system in real-world scenarios.

Research discovery: the legacy system is not important and should be ignored completely. Instead, defining the users' specific actions and physical touchpoints will better inform the design timeline

Ideas

Personal paper sketches

I start with paper sketches and treat them like private notes. It's akin to playing in a personal sandbox with no expectations and lots of intuition. I draw out others' ideas and translate conversations into components for my own understanding.

"Viewing items"

"Seleting parcels"

"Dragging and dropping"

Draft designs are for agreement

Product Owners think about the product as much as users value the tasks it's meant to support; it's their baby, and aligning the initial interface with POs' and stakeholders' expectations and vision is often more important early on.

Teamwork insight: I've broken down the interaction into 3 tasks and identified the visual flow of information. By guiding my PO step-by-step, we were able to come to an agreement on the final interface.

Flow charts define UI components

Task statements define UI components of the interface and each statement maps to standardized universal UI component(s).

"The warehouse operator parcel dimensions."
→ Editable component
"Quantity is physically checked against online data..."
→ Search toolbar
"Printing labels need to include the following information..."
→ Setup wizard

Design discovery: By applying the user journey map framework as a guide, I discovered that I could define components and organize and track their progress while setting my junior designer up for success.

Outcome after four weeks

Less of a "solution", more of a foundation

From past experience I've learned presentations are often assumed as final solutions, especially to those unfamiliar with the design process. So directing the conversation around the concept of the design was just as important as showing the interface "solution" screens.

I spoke to how components were essentially parts of features that should be introduced incrementally (hover over each screen below to see the priority of the components).

Signed on to lead the design road map

Through these screens, I was able to show the team why designing incrementally was the the best approach and that having a "read to build" solution would not be efficient. As a result of this presentation and my emphasis on following a human-centric design process, I was hired to lead the design for the entire project.



Screen 1: Viewing all shipments in the system
This screen below may appear simple but an engineer would tell you otherwise. All of these screens needed to be accessible at 3 different levels of permission to address the needs of 3 different user groups.



Screen 2: Shipping information for a specific order
Each screen is dedicated to showing components required for the application to function as a minimum viable product.

For instance, this screen shows the most complicated items of a shipment (e.g. items that need to be at a certain temperature while being transported) that need to be addressed in order to function as an MVP.



Screen 3: Editing parcel information within a shipment of multiple parcels
By discussing components in detail, I was able to show the team just how much work was involved to produce each screen and why designing incrementally was the the best approach.

Despite my confidence in knowing what I was presenting, there were aspects of the design that I didn't anticipate being a challenge.

For example, this particular screen would become a UI challenge because I wasn't able to assess the complexity of the features accurately beforehand (more on this later).
